⚠️ What goes wrong with just a tag
You can filter policies by "GDPR" — but GDPR itself knows nothing about your data.
GDPR has no idea which tables, columns or processes it covers. You have to manually check.
When an auditor asks "show me everything covered under GDPR" — you have to go search for it yourself.
Nothing automatically happens when you add the GDPR tag. No tasks. No checks. No forms.

What actually changes when GDPR is an object
1
You can ask "what is under GDPR?"
With a tag: you search every policy and asset for the string "GDPR". Manual. Slow.

With an object: you open the GDPR page and it already lists everything linked to it — all tables, all policies, all processes. One click.
2
Linking triggers something real
With a tag: nothing happens when you add "GDPR".

With an object: the moment you create a complies_with relationship between a table and GDPR — tasks fire. The data owner gets a DPIA form. The steward gets a masking check task. Article 30 register gets a new entry.
3
Compliance % becomes calculable
With a tag: how many assets are GDPR compliant? You don't know. You'd have to check manually.

With an object: GDPR knows exactly how many things are linked to it and whether each one has completed the required tasks. So it can show: 47 assets, 32 compliant = 68%.
The simplest way to think about it
🏷️ GDPR as a Tag
Like writing "GDPR" on a sticky note and putting it on a folder.
✗ GDPR doesn't know about the folder
✗ The folder doesn't know about other GDPR folders
✗ Removing the sticky note is all you need to "remove" GDPR
✗ Nothing happens when you put the sticky note on
GDPR is passive. It's just a label you read.
📋 GDPR as an Object
Like hiring a GDPR compliance officer who sits in your system.
✓ They know every table, policy, process under their watch
✓ When you add something new, they hand you a checklist
✓ They track whether every item on that list is done
✓ They can tell an auditor exactly what's covered and what isn't
GDPR is active. It watches, tracks, and assigns work.
💡
So to directly answer your question: In Collibra, GDPR is linked to assets through a named relationship — not a tag, not a free-text field. The relationship has a type (e.g. "complies_with", "governed_by", "subject_to") and it goes both ways. From the GDPR page you can see every asset linked to it. From an asset's page you can see every regulation it falls under. In our prototype right now, we store GDPR as a string in an array on the policy — which is the tag approach. It's the right starting point. The upgrade path is: make Regulation its own entity, define the relationship types, and let the system traverse those relationships — that's when GDPR becomes active instead of passive. That's a Regulations module — a Phase 2 feature.
